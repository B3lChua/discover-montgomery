    Welcome

    The Story of Montgomery

    Montgomery, Alabama is a city where history isn't behind glass in a museum. It lives in the streets, the churches, the courthouses, and the people. From its founding as a river town in 1819 to its role as the birthplace of the Civil Rights Movement, Montgomery has always been a city where the course of a nation changed.

    This is a city of firsts: the first capital of the Confederacy, the first electrically powered streetcar system in the Western Hemisphere, and the first mass action of the modern Civil Rights Movement. Those contradictions aren't a flaw. They're the point.

    1955 – 1956

    The Montgomery Bus Boycott

    On December 1, 1955, Rosa Parks refused to give up her seat on a Montgomery city bus. What followed was a 381-day boycott that galvanized the nation and launched a 26-year-old pastor named Martin Luther King Jr. into the national spotlight.

    The boycott wasn't spontaneous. It was organized, strategic, and sustained by tens of thousands of ordinary people who walked to work, carpooled, and sacrificed for over a year. It ended when the Supreme Court ruled that segregated buses were unconstitutional.

    1961

    The Freedom Riders

    In May 1961, Freedom Riders arrived at Montgomery's Greyhound bus station, testing the Supreme Court's ruling against segregated interstate travel. They were met with violence, but they didn't stop. The Riders' courage forced the federal government to enforce desegregation.

    Freedom Rides Museum

    The restored Greyhound station where the Riders arrived. Now a museum documenting their courage.

    View on map →

    First Baptist Church

    Where 1,500 people gathered with Dr. King as a mob surrounded the church. U.S. Marshals intervened.

    1965

    Selma to Montgomery Marches

    The 54-mile march from Selma to Montgomery for voting rights ended on the steps of the Alabama State Capitol on March 25, 1965. Dr. King addressed 25,000 marchers. Five months later, President Johnson signed the Voting Rights Act.

    Alabama State Capitol

    The endpoint of the march. The bronze star on the steps marks where Jefferson Davis stood, and where King triumphed.

    Civil Rights Memorial

    Designed by Maya Lin (who designed the Vietnam Veterans Memorial). Water flows over the names of 40 martyrs.

    Today

    Montgomery Now

    Montgomery is a city in motion. Downtown is experiencing a renaissance, with new restaurants, businesses, and cultural venues transforming historic buildings. The National Memorial for Peace and Justice, opened in 2018, confronts the legacy of racial terror and has drawn visitors from around the world.

    The city's data tells a story of growth: new business licenses are outpacing closures in key corridors, construction permits are up, and 311 responsiveness is improving. But the data also reveals work still to be done: perception gaps between how safe neighborhoods actually are and how they're portrayed, underutilized public spaces, and corridors waiting for investment.

    Discover Montgomery was built to make that data visible, accessible, and human. Because a city isn't just its numbers. It's its story.

    National Memorial for Peace and Justice

    The nation's first memorial dedicated to victims of lynching. A profound and essential experience.
